10–15 minutes is not a presentation slot.

It’s a **curiosity expansion slot**.

Your goal is NOT to explain the whole framework.
Your goal is to:

1. Create tension
2. Show structured thinking
3. Demonstrate credibility
4. Leave them wanting a deeper session

Think of it as:
**Problem → Reframe → Example → Implication → Ask**

Here’s a tight structure you can follow.

---

# Minute 0–2: Start with Tension (Make Them Lean In)

Open with something like:

> We treat data projects as delivery initiatives — not as capital allocation decisions.
> That’s why ROI conversations always feel vague.

Pause.

Then:

> If I asked today: what is the expected economic value of our last three data projects — could we answer confidently?

Let silence do the work.

---

# Minute 2–5: Reframe the Entire Game

Now introduce the core idea:

> Data creates value only when it changes a decision.
> If no decision changes, there is no economic value.

Then introduce the shift:

Instead of asking:

* What dashboard?
* What model?
* What pipeline?

Ask:

* What exact decision is being improved?
* How often is it made?
* What is the baseline?
* What economic lever moves — revenue, cost, or risk?

Now you’ve moved from tech to economics.

---

# Minute 5–9: Use One Strong Example (DSAR is ideal)

Say:

> Take DSAR automation.
> 30–40 requests per day.
> 30-day SLA.
> Regulatory exposure if breached.

Then walk them through:

Baseline:

* Manual processing
* SLA breach probability
* Executive time cost

After automation:

* Reduced breach probability
* Lower operational cost
* Scalable compliance

Then say the key line:

> That’s not a compliance tool.
> That’s a risk-adjusted expected value improvement across 12,000+ annual decision events.

Now you’ve shown it’s not theory.

---

# Minute 9–12: Introduce the Framework (Lightly)

Don’t go deep.

Just say:

> I’ve structured this into a simple 6-step loop:
>
> 1. Define the decision
> 2. Quantify baseline
> 3. Identify economic lever
> 4. Estimate uplift per decision
> 5. Multiply by frequency
> 6. Compare against cost

Then add:

> It’s basically intrinsic valuation applied to decision systems.

That elevates the conversation intellectually.

---

# Minute 12–14: Strategic Implication

Now zoom out.

> If we institutionalized this:
>
> * We’d filter ornamental AI.
> * We’d prioritize high-frequency, high-leverage decisions.
> * We’d defend budgets more credibly.
> * We’d treat data investments like investments.

This is where they start thinking.

---

# Final Minute: The Ask

Keep it simple:

> I’m not proposing training yet.
> I’d just like to pressure-test this thinking with you and maybe pilot it on one upcoming initiative.

You’re asking for collaboration, not approval.

---

# What NOT To Do

* Don’t open slides.
* Don’t over-explain probability math.
* Don’t sound like you’re criticizing past projects.
* Don’t position it as a “course” yet.

This is a leadership conversation, not a workshop.

---

# The Psychological Target

When you leave the room, you want them thinking:

“He’s not talking about dashboards.
He’s talking about capital discipline.”

That’s executive language.
